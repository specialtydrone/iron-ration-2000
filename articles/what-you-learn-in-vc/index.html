What You Learn In VC

It’s been about five months since my two-year tour in venture capital ended and about three weeks since the 3D printing company I joined afterwards was hastily dissolved by its parent company.

For anyone affected by the recent wave of tech layoffs, it’s been “interesting” times.

While I’ve yet to fully grapple with my recent funemployment, I do think enough time has passed that I can speak to some of the career-shaping experiences from my adventure in private equity.

A Team to Die For

In Kitchen Confidential, Anthony Bourdain describes the staff of a rival kitchen whose cohesion, intimacy, and ability to operate with military precision in the midst of the dinner rush took on a mythological status to his young self:

“They were richer, more confident, and moved with even more swagger and style than our motley crew of oddballs and amateurs. They moved in a pack, with their own dialect - a high-pitched, ultra-femme, affected drawl, salted with terms from eighteenth-century English literature and Marine Corps drill instructor-speak - a lush, intimidating, sardonic secret language, which was much imitated.”

That was us. That was my team.

You can’t fully appreciate an effective team until you work on one. Many of us are on teams in the sense that we share the same tier on an org chart. But until you are in the trenches, shoulder-to-shoulder with other talented creatives, cranking copy and fine-tuning designs to meticulous levels on insane deadlines, you cannot say you’ve served on an effective team.

This is the aspect of my time in VC that will most likely never be replicated because it required the right personalities, the right projects, and the right environment to exist. Much like a band in its prime, before the inevitable implosion, we simply gelled and produced effortless hits.

Bullpen gang 4 life. You know who you are.

Companies as Cuts of Beef

In private equity and VC specifically, you learn to look at businesses the way a butcher looks at a cut of beef or a mechanic looks at a car they’re working on. You look beyond the customer-facing elements of an organization and start asking foundational questions.

What’s the company’s runway? Any defensive moats or patented IP? Who would immediately benefit from their acquisition? Who’s on their board? Who have they poached for their engineering team? Is their founder ex-FAANG? Any Ivy League affinities? How’s the founder in the room versus their pre-recorded pitch? How sticky is their product? Does their founder have an in with a government agency?

It is impossible for me to look at companies the way I did before.

Back when I was a writer and reporter, I experienced a similar perspective realignment. You can never read a press release or hear a canned soundbite the way you were meant to experience them. You immediately see the artifice of such things. You know the playbooks they’re trying to follow, the companies they’re emulating, and the very mechanical way such things are meant to function.

My time in VC and journalism have permanently altered the way I view the world and my work.

Vision Versus Reality

You can dress up the mechanics of venture capital however you want but it is fundamentally speculation.

Startups are formed with business plans that require capital to realize. VCs offer them capital in exchange for stake in the company. Both parties are banking on the existence of future profits with zero guarantees they will materialize. Nonetheless, when the deal is closed, there are handshakes, the uncorking of champagne bottles, and astronomical expectations are put in place with statistically little chance of being met.

My realist gut is locked in an endless struggle with the hopeful part of my brain that believes the proliferation of technology can and should be used to ease suffering where it can and benefit the most amount of people possible.

For that to happen, we need teams with the ability to see the world the way it could be and have a plan — however far of a stretch — to achieve it.

Of course, for every Apple or SpaceX, there are thousands of failed startups that both literally and figuratively detonated on the launchpad. For every honest and competent CEO, there are manifold con artists and grifters.

It is this tension between the reality of cold hard numbers — an investment’s returns — and the optimism required to make a multi-million dollar bet on yet another thin clone of Uber or DoorDash that sticks with me.

In some circles, VCs are regarded as vultures, clueless speculators, and parasites. Yet it is hard to argue that the bets they’ve made haven’t fundamentally reshaped the world.

Life After Private Equity

It’s been hard to stop writing about computer-brain interfaces, commercial rocket launches, billion-dollar acquisitions, and quantum computer startups. Which isn’t to say that I necessarily miss the life-and-death, make-or-break nature of the work. But for all of its demands, it was exciting.

In an age when the entirety of the earth has been mapped down to the millimeter by a probably-VC-backed startup, when most people only experience adventures on the screens of their phones or game consoles, my time in venture capital proved to be my own unexpected journey. It was an antidote to the straightforward-but-necessary data sheets, technical specifications, and Salesforce dashboards of my former professional life.

I’m not sure what’s on the horizon for me. Whatever my next role is, I hope to bring my love of technology, programming, procedural generation, and content creation to the table.

If you know of any organization looking for expertise in these areas and wants to hire a specialist with my skillset, let me know.
