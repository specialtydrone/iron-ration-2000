It's been about five months since my two-year tour in venture capital
ended and about three weeks since the 3D printing company I joined
afterwards was [hastily
dissolved](https://3dprint.com/306731/prodways-to-discontinue-solidscape-wax-3d-printers-for-jewelry/)
by its parent company.

For anyone affected by the [recent
wave](https://www.npr.org/2024/01/28/1227326215/nearly-25-000-tech-workers-laid-off-in-the-first-weeks-of-2024-whats-going-on)
of tech layoffs, it's been "interesting" times.

While I've yet to fully grapple with my recent funemployment, I do think
enough time has passed that I can speak to some of the career-shaping
experiences from my adventure in private equity.

## A Team to Die For

![The
Bear](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E12AQH7uzMmA7Atyw/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/0/1705611656709?e=1782345600&v=beta&t=BZTIFiNjbXe25-R3tXDRUdl37lkTNcK2JkHg3fjRKU8){media-urn="urn:li:digitalmediaAsset:D4E12AQH7uzMmA7Atyw"}

In *Kitchen Confidential*, Anthony Bourdain describes the staff of a
rival kitchen whose cohesion, intimacy, and ability to operate with
military precision in the midst of the dinner rush took on a
mythological status to his young self:

> "They were richer, more confident, and moved with even more swagger
> and style than our motley crew of oddballs and amateurs. They moved in
> a pack, with their own dialect - a high-pitched, ultra-femme, affected
> drawl, salted with terms from eighteenth-century English literature
> and Marine Corps drill instructor-speak - a lush, intimidating,
> sardonic secret language, which was much imitated."

That was us. That was my team.

You can't fully appreciate an effective team until you work on one. Many
of us are on teams in the sense that we share the same tier on an org
chart. But until you are in the trenches, shoulder-to-shoulder with
other talented creatives, cranking copy and fine-tuning designs to
meticulous levels on insane deadlines, you cannot say you've served on
an effective team.

This is the aspect of my time in VC that will most likely never be
replicated because it required the right personalities, the right
projects, and the right environment to exist. Much like a band in its
prime, before the inevitable implosion, we simply gelled and produced
effortless hits.

Bullpen gang 4 life. You know who you are.

## Companies as Cuts of Beef

![Gangs of New
York](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E12AQErCBsBzGyLbA/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/0/1707323820492?e=1782345600&v=beta&t=-IvFzsZBBdIA37jnLz44KO8ysBja13cgo1XnMyfT5EQ){media-urn="urn:li:digitalmediaAsset:D4E12AQErCBsBzGyLbA"}

In private equity and VC specifically, you learn to look at businesses
the way a butcher looks at a cut of beef or a mechanic looks at a car
they're working on. You look beyond the customer-facing elements of an
organization and start asking foundational questions.

What's the company's runway? Any defensive moats or patented IP? Who
would immediately benefit from their acquisition? Who's on their board?
Who have they poached for their engineering team? Is their founder
ex-FAANG? Any Ivy League affinities? How's the founder [in the
room](https://youtu.be/N6Zz-Nkkaxc?si=gPoSu0NMc4T2Mgl7&t=26)
versus their pre-recorded pitch? How sticky is their product? Does their
founder have an in with a government agency?

It is impossible for me to look at companies the way I did before.

Back when I was a writer and reporter, I experienced a similar
perspective realignment. You can never read a press release or hear a
canned soundbite the way you were meant to experience them. You
immediately see the artifice of such things. You know the playbooks
they're trying to follow, the companies they're emulating, and the very
mechanical way such things are meant to function.

My time in VC and journalism have permanently altered the way I view the
world and my work.

### Vision Versus Reality

![Apple Vision
Pro](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E12AQEXrPLDeKvzpg/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/0/1707490990050?e=1782345600&v=beta&t=Al6RAJvg0jdk_PRXwmfGJNr5YAED0rnmHAnUZ2BLB68){media-urn="urn:li:digitalmediaAsset:D4E12AQEXrPLDeKvzpg"}

You can dress up the mechanics of venture capital however you want but
it is fundamentally speculation.

Startups are formed with business plans that require capital to realize.
VCs offer them capital in exchange for stake in the company. Both
parties are banking on the existence of future profits with zero
guarantees they will materialize. Nonetheless, when the deal is closed,
there are handshakes, the uncorking of champagne bottles, and
astronomical expectations are put in place with [statistically little
chance](https://www.fastcompany.com/3003827/why-most-venture-backed-companies-fail)
of being met.

My realist gut is locked in an endless struggle with the hopeful part of
my brain that believes the proliferation of technology can and should be
used to ease suffering where it can and benefit the most amount of
people possible.

For that to happen, we need teams with the ability to see the world the
way it could be and have a plan --- however far of a stretch --- to
achieve it.

Of course, for every Apple or SpaceX, there are thousands of failed
startups that both literally and figuratively detonated on the
launchpad. For every honest and competent CEO, there are manifold [con
artists](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/bitconnect-founder-indicted-global-24-billion-cryptocurrency-scheme)
and grifters.

It is this tension between the reality of cold hard numbers --- an
investment's returns --- and the optimism required to make a
multi-million dollar bet on yet another thin clone of Uber or DoorDash
that sticks with me.

In some circles, VCs are regarded as vultures, [clueless
speculators](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2022-11-14/sequoia-ftx-profile-of-sam-bankman-fried-sbf-was-a-face-plant?embedded-checkout=true),
and parasites. Yet it is hard to argue that the bets they've made
haven't fundamentally reshaped the world.

## Life After Private Equity

![Beetlejuice](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E12AQGe3vBqC4QrZQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/0/1707325291743?e=1782345600&v=beta&t=fuQSF59VmsvACBAZMmgAGzQyUPjJc53Wghat9DJ-CWQ){media-urn="urn:li:digitalmediaAsset:D4E12AQGe3vBqC4QrZQ"}

It's been hard to stop writing about computer-brain interfaces,
commercial rocket launches, billion-dollar acquisitions, and quantum
computer startups. Which isn't to say that I necessarily miss the
life-and-death, make-or-break nature of the work. But for all of its
demands, it was exciting.

In an age when the entirety of the earth has been mapped down to the
millimeter by a probably-VC-backed startup, when most people only
experience adventures on the screens of their phones or game consoles,
my time in venture capital proved to be my own unexpected journey. It
was an antidote to the straightforward-but-necessary data sheets,
technical specifications, and Salesforce dashboards of my former
professional life.

I'm not sure what's on the horizon for me. Whatever my next role is, I
hope to bring my love of technology, programming, procedural generation,
and content creation to the table.

**If you know of any organization looking for expertise in these areas
and wants to hire a specialist with my skillset, let me know.**
