Wandering the Land of the Brand-less

A long-time friend and fellow marketer of mine recently told me: “You have no brand.” I’d been lamenting the job market, how poorly timed my springtime departure was from my previous gig, and the general listlessness I felt about the post-Covid world we now inhabit. “My brand,” I replied, “is that I have no brand.” The get-a-load-of-this-guy laugh on the other end of the line confirmed the absurdity of my claim.

Do mid-level marketers and copywriters need a personal brand to succeed? The expectations certainly exist, especially browsing my LinkedIn feed. I’m not what you’d call an enthusiastic user of Microsoft’s career-oriented social network; I’m put off by the sheer amount of posts crammed with low-traffic hashtags, unsolicited motivational advice, and calls to engage with content about generating more engagement with content in a post with little engagement itself-- perhaps I’m simply not used to it, but it all seems positively bankrupt of practical value.

A bit about me: I’ve worked at two ad agencies, an old media newsroom, and at a custom software developer. Roughly ten years and change of seasoned content creation. I’ve been sworn at by editors, ghostwritten long-form pieces in dying magazines, pitched companies large and small, and written thousands of words hours before deadlines.

In that decade of authoring effective content, I have never said to my manager or editor “Don’t worry. My personal brand will carry this project over the finish line.” Because, in reality, the content needs to stand on its own. An essay needs to win over its readers. A whitepaper needs to deal in facts and make a compelling argument. Calls to action must be effectively deployed. Website content must be easily navigated and accessible. All of it must be proofread to within an inch of its life. In short: The content you are creating must function independently of its author.

I’m not discounting the power of a loyal and engaged following. Influencers have fundamentally changed marketing. But look no further than another Microsoft product, Mixer, to see the limits of personal brands. The now-defunct streaming service poached arguably the most popular streamer alive, Ninja, from Twitch to shore up its stable of creators. Millions of dollars changed hands. Press releases went out. Mixer failed anyway, and the net beneficiary of all of this was Ninja and meme creators.

Look. Could I stand to put myself out there a little more? Sure. You don’t create things for a living without dabbling in the dark arts of self-promotion. But is a personal brand a necessary substitute for skills, experience, and the ability to adapt? No, I don’t believe it is.

At least for me, my strength is speaking for others. Finding their voices, finding a tone, identifying what to say and how to say it; translating ideas into meaningful content that only betrays my fingerprints upon close scrutiny. If I’m doing my job correctly, what I write will stick in your brain and you’ll never question who wrote it or why. I take the form of whatever brand I need to write for because that’s what the vast majority of organizations want.

My brand is that I have no brand.
