In a week or so, my first child will hopefully arrive in this world with
little drama but much fanfare. A writer by trade, I've already chosen
what my first words to her will be, though the reality is I already
speak to her every night while my wife and I watch schlocky TV on the
couch. My daughter's full-term now. In fact, she walloped me the other
night as I rested my hand on my wife's belly, making me a tragic
statistic of unborn-infant-on-parent violence. All that's left for my
wife and I to do is wait. But in finding ways to kill time, I'm
discovering my impending fatherhood reshaping how I consume media.

I've been playing video games since the day my father brought home a
used, commercially ill fated Sega Master System. But it wasn't until he
lugged an outrageously priced Tandy 1000 RL IBM-clone and a box of
bootleg floppy disks into our apartment that I fell into a lifelong love
affair with hyper-niche PC games.

People my age typically identify Super Mario as their first formative
gaming experience. For me, it was staring at a monochrome DOS prompt,
inserting Sharpy-scrawled diskettes into one of the drives, then trying
to type the right command to bring the display to life. One disk would
whisk me off to Chicago's Meigs Field in Microsoft's Flight Simulator
4.0. Another tasked me with founding and managing a metropolis in
SimCity. As a small child, I couldn't fully appreciate any of these
games like the adults they were designed for. Nonetheless, I understood
that successfully landing a Cessna or leading an urban renewal
initiative was vastly more engaging than stomping mushroom-shaped goons
and rescuing a princess.

At present, I own an embarrassing amount of games to help me while the
hours away until my daughter arrives. Yet the idea of passing time by
murdering hordes of virtual enemies in order to fill a meter or driving
arbitrary numbers higher have lost their appeal. I've already spent an
incalculable amount of my youth slaying ghouls, dispatching other
players, and leveling up characters I've long since forgotten the names
of. My mind is turning back to those hyper niche DOS games, the kind
that came directly out of a designer's mind and manifested on the screen
with little corporate oversight or meddling. What is the antithesis of a
Fortnite or Call of Duty?

The answer, it turns out, is Hideo Kojima's Death Stranding.

I'm not going to bore you with an in-depth explanation of the game;
plenty of digital ink has already been spilled about it. Suffice to say
it's about delivering packages in a United States shattered by a
catastrophic paranormal event in the vein of Leftovers mashed up with
Poltergeist and the Postman. The remaining population is spread out and
isolated both geographically and informationally. Your objective is to
reunite the country not through violence, but by delivering cargo
between humanity's disparate enclaves and connecting them to the game's
equivalent of the internet.

Where Grand Theft Auto entices you to commit wanton destruction in its
lavishly detailed worlds and Fortnite demands you be the last person
standing, Death Stranding asks its players to cooperatively rebuild a
country and its infrastructure. It's as if I rummaged around in that box
of old floppies and found a game that was impossible to discuss at grade
school recess without being called a liar because its premise is such a
departure from "kill things/get points."

As a testament to Death Stranding's design and themes, I don't feel
guilty playing it as a father-to-be. Is it as intellectually challenging
as working through Infinite Jest or writing a novel? Absolutely not. But
building a road network to allow other players to effortlessly cruise
over terrain my character and I struggled over is its own reward.
Reconnecting families and communities torn apart by disaster by climbing
inhospitable mountains and fording rivers with hundreds of pounds on
your character's back feels triumphant in a way that hammering a trigger
on your controller to gun down your opponents is not.

In Death Stranding's gameplay, I see the values I hope my daughter will
internalize and embody: Building and maintaining things rather than
tearing them down. Uniting people through mutual interests instead of
dividing them for short-term political advantage. Acknowledging that,
despite the hyper-volatile partisan climate we find ourselves in today,
we are ultimately connected to and dependent on each other whether we
like it or not. That violence alone cannot solve humanity's problems.
That the relative freedom we enjoy in the United States exists not
solely because of the Revolutionary War, but because the Constitution's
architects overcame their many, many differences to build an institution
they hoped would endure for centuries.

It may seem like I'm reaching, but these themes are raised time and time
again in Hideo Kojima's work. Almost all of his titles, going back to
the '80s feature an anti-nuclear weapon angle, both in their stories and
gameplay mechanics. This is a game designer who has never shied away
from questioning what a soldier's role is, both on the battlefield and
at home. In 2001's Metal Gear Solid 2: Sons of Liberty, he famously
explored how digital disinformation could be weaponized to subvert
democratic societies, blur the line between fiction and reality, and
empower tyrannical individuals to rise to power. The past half decade
has proved Kojima's vision to be prophetic.

As my wife rightly points out, when my daughter arrives, the concepts of
nation states, the Constitution, and even object permanence will be
foreign to her. Really, she'll be focused on, you know, getting enough
milk to survive, balancing her body temperature, and being sufficiently
cuddled. Even just typing this out right now, I'm overcome with the
desire to just meet her and see her face. The values will come later.

Her father will continue to philosophize, of course, while he points at
whatever screen he's in front of, decrying the lack of meaning in
whatever's passing for mainstream digital entertainment. "Kojima had it
all figured out way back in '19," he'll say, while his daughter masters
primary colors and walking in straight lines. "With DOS, you had to put
in these things called floppies," he'll explain to her as she fumbles
with her Duplo blocks. "Games aren't just about making points go up,
they can convey nuanced meaning on par with literature" he'll later
argue as she rolls her eyes and resignedly says "Yes dad, I know."
